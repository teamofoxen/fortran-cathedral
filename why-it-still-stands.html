Why It Still Stands

Fortran is not standing because nobody got around to demolishing it. It is standing because it is load-bearing. The language holds up a measurable share of the numerical software that forecasts weather, models climate, simulates aircraft, and runs on the world's largest computers — and it holds that weight for reasons that are concrete, unromantic, and worth understanding.

This page states those reasons plainly, with sources. Where an authoritative source could not be found, the claim was not made.

Myth and reality

Four common myths, answered
MythReality
Fortran is dead.The ISO standard has been revised continuously through Fortran 2023[1], and at least three major compilers are under active development[2].
Fortran means FORTRAN 77.Modern Fortran is free-form, modular, and checked. See the exhibits in Old Testament / Modern Testament, where both liturgies compile.

Pillar I: arrays as first-class citizens

Fortran's subject matter is the array. Multidimensional arrays are part of the language's core grammar, and since Fortran 90 they converse directly: whole-array arithmetic, array constructors, and allocatable arrays sized at run time[14]. Code written this way says what the mathematics means, and leaves the compiler an unusually clear view of what may be optimized.

Pillar II: mature optimizing compilers

A language is only as alive as its compilers. Fortran has several under active development: GFortran within GCC[3], Intel's LLVM-based ifx with Fortran 2018 support and selected 2023 features[4], and LLVM Flang[5], with a wider field catalogued by the community[2]. Decades of investment in these optimizers is a compounding asset that a rewrite forfeits on day one.

Pillar III: validated code is capital

Scientific code is not merely written; it is validated — against experiments, observations, and decades of operational use. The standard retires features with unusual care, as the Testaments wing shows, and compilers keep faith with old scripture. The result: numerical code validated over generations still compiles, and institutions do not casually discard capital of that kind.

Pillar IV: the foundations are Fortran

The reference implementation of LAPACK — the linear-algebra package beneath a great deal of numerical software — is written in Fortran 90 and published at Netlib[6]. Higher ecosystems reach down to it: SciPy documents its own low-level LAPACK interfaces[7]. And the traffic flows both ways — standard C interoperability (iso_c_binding) makes Fortran kernels callable from the wider world[8]. The Book of BLAS treats these foundations at length.

Pillar V: institutional continuity

The institutions that predict and simulate the physical world say plainly what their systems are built from. ECMWF describes the IFS suite as written primarily in modern Fortran[9] and offers OpenIFS to research institutions[10]. NCAR's CESM climate model is written mostly in Fortran[11], and the WRF atmospheric model's codebase is predominantly Fortran[12]. NASA catalogues FUN3D, a Navier–Stokes solver used across aeronautics, as written in Fortran[13]. Weather, climate, aerospace, and the machines they run on: this is the continuity that keeps the language funded, taught, and compiled.

The unbroken line

Nine revisions of one standard, each carrying the old code forward[1]:

  1. FORTRAN 1957
  2. FORTRAN 66 1966
  3. FORTRAN 77 1978
  4. Fortran 90 1991
  5. Fortran 95 1997
  6. Fortran 2003 2004
  7. Fortran 2008 2010
  8. Fortran 2018 2018
  9. Fortran 2023 2023
