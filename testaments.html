Old Testament / Modern Testament

The stereotype of Fortran is a fixed-form deck from 1977: capital letters, numbered statements, columns ruled like a ledger. The stereotype is real — and it is also a museum piece. Fortran has been revised by international standard nine times, and the modern language is free-form, modular, and checked. This wing reads the two side by side.

Every exhibit below is real code from this repository, read from disk by the generator, dressed by a Fortran syntax highlighter, and syntax-checked by GFortran during forty validate. Every verse compiles.

The shape of the source

Fixed form is a punched card wearing a standard: comment marks in column 1, statement labels in columns 1–5, a continuation mark in column 6, statements confined to columns 7 through 72, and silence beyond. Free source form, introduced with Fortran 90, released the line from the card; code begins where it is needed, ! opens a comment, and & continues.

Loops and labels

The old loop marched to a numbered statement. The block do … end do of Fortran 90, with exit and cycle, replaced the numbers with structure. The labeled DO is obsolescent under Fortran 2018; its shared-termination cousins — and the arithmetic IF — were deleted from that standard outright, though compilers still accept them for the sake of the old scriptures.

Arrays, element by element or whole

Arrays are the reason Fortran survives, and Fortran 90 let them speak for themselves: whole-array arithmetic, array constructors, and allocatable arrays sized at run time. What the Old Testament wrote as three loops, the Modern Testament writes as three sentences of algebra — which is also what the compiler optimizers prefer to read.

Sharing data

The COMMON block shared raw storage by position: every program unit redeclared it, and no one checked their agreement. The module, introduced with Fortran 90, shares by name through an explicit, compiler-checked interface. COMMON, EQUIVALENCE, and BLOCK DATA were declared obsolescent in Fortran 2018; the module is why nobody mourns.

Precision, by decree or by name

DOUBLE PRECISION has been standard since the early books, and the widely seen REAL*8 never was — a vendor dialect mistaken for scripture. Fortran 90 brought selected_real_kind; Fortran 2008 added the named kind constants real32, real64, and real128 in iso_fortran_env, so precision is requested by meaning rather than by folklore.

The living standard

Nine revisions, one unbroken line. Years give the publication of each standard; the first entry is the language's delivery by IBM.

  1. FORTRAN 1957
  2. FORTRAN 66 1966
  3. FORTRAN 77 1978
  4. Fortran 90 1991
  5. Fortran 95 1997
  6. Fortran 2003 2004
  7. Fortran 2008 2010
  8. Fortran 2018 2018
  9. Fortran 2023 2023

The standard retires its past with care: features are first declared obsolescent (still standard, discouraged), and only rarely deleted. Compilers, serving decades of working scripture, generally accept even the deleted. That patience is not weakness. It is why fifty-year-old physics still compiles.
